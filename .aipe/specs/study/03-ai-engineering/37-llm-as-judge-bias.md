# LLM-as-judge bias

**Industry name(s):** LLM-as-judge bias, position bias, verbosity bias, self-preference bias
**Type:** Industry standard

> The judge isn't a person — it's another LLM, and it has predictable wrong-shaped preferences you must control for.

**See also:** → [36-eval-methods](./36-eval-methods.md) · → [35-eval-set-types](./35-eval-set-types.md)

---

## Why care

Open Vercel's A/B test framework and look at how it controls for position bias. If variant A is always shown first and variant B second, the click-through rate on A overstates A's real lift by 5–15% — users default to the first option presented. Vercel's framework randomises variant order per session, then averages results across the two orderings. GitHub Copilot Chat's "rate this completion" UI faces the same trap and addresses it the same way. PostHog's experiment system ships position-randomisation by default for the same reason. None of these biases are corrupt — they're systematic preferences that leak into ratings whenever the rater (human or LLM) sees options in a fixed order, and the production fix is always order-swap-plus-averaging.

The implicit question is "what systematic preferences does the judging instrument bring that aren't actually about quality?" LLM-as-judge biases are the answer: position bias (favours whichever output appears first, 5–15%), verbosity bias (favours longer outputs even when filler, 10–20%), self-preference bias (favours outputs from its own model family, 5–10%). Production eval pipelines name them and apply controls; lazy pipelines treat the judge as ground truth and ship the bias as a quality regression.

**What depends on getting this right:** whether prompt-change deltas are actual quality lifts or measurement artefacts, and whether shipped outputs feel like what users want vs what the judge happens to like. For loopd the planned controls land in `scripts/eval-harness/metrics/rubricJudge.ts` and `pairwise.ts` for `[B3.3]` (caption) and `[B3.7]` (aipe pairwise) — position swap with averaged scores, "concise" as an explicit rubric criterion, and cross-model judging using the already-abstracted `getProvider()` to swap GPT-4o in when caption is generated by Sonnet. Without the controls, a "12% quality lift" on caption could be 6% bias plus 6% real signal — and the shipped prompt biases caption variants toward verbose, sycophantic, self-congratulatory shape.

Without bias controls:
- Pairwise judge sees (A, B) once → "A wins" 60% of the time because A is shown first; "improvement" is half position bias
- Rubric weights "thoroughness" without "concise" → judge rewards a 200-word caption variant with the same content as an 80-word one; loopd's deliberately-short captions get downscored
- Sonnet judges Sonnet captions → self-preference inflates Sonnet outputs ~5–10% over the GPT-4o-generated baseline; switch providers later and the eval refuses to validate the new prompt

With bias controls:
- Position swap: every pairwise runs twice with reversed order; average the two; if A wins position-1 and loses position-2, the apparent preference was bias — record as tie. Cross-position variance ≤ 5% as a sanity check
- Verbosity: "concise" is an explicit rubric criterion alongside "fits the mood / avoids forbidden patterns / non-repetitive / specific to the entry"; judge balances length against content
- Self-preference: caption generated on Sonnet → judged by GPT-4o; the provider-abstracted chain layer makes this a config flip in `[B3.3]`
- Judge-human calibration on 20 outputs first — Spearman correlation or per-criterion concordance ≥ 80% before trusting the judge for ship decisions
- Cost paid: roughly 2× judge calls per eval run (position swap doubles pairwise volume), ~$1–2 per full eval instead of $0.50–1

The judge isn't a person — it's a measurement instrument with its own preferences; control them or ship them.

## How it works

A judge LLM, given `(input, output, rubric)`, returns a score. The score is not "the quality" — it's "the judge's score of the quality." The two correlate but differ in three known directions.

### Bias 1: Position bias

When the judge sees two outputs side-by-side ("which is better, A or B?"), it systematically prefers whichever is shown *first*. Studies have measured this preference at 5-15% across different judge models.

If you're coming from frontend, this is the same shape as A/B test results being biased by which option appears on top of the page. The model has a "default to the first thing" prior.

**Control:** run every pairwise comparison twice with positions swapped. Average the two scores. If A wins position-1 and loses position-2, treat as a tie — the apparent preference was position bias, not quality.

### Bias 2: Verbosity bias

Judges systematically prefer longer outputs. A 200-word response with the same content as an 80-word response often scores higher on rubric criteria like "thorough" or "specific" even when the extra words are filler. Measured at 10-20% in some benchmarks.

**Control:** include "concise" as an explicit rubric criterion. The judge then scores brevity as a positive, and the bias self-corrects.

For loopd: caption variants are deliberately short (one line each). Without a concise criterion, the judge might rate longer captions as "more thorough" — wrong direction for caption.

### Bias 3: Self-preference bias

A judge tends to prefer outputs generated by models in its own family — Sonnet judging Sonnet output rates it ~5-10% higher than identical-quality output from GPT-4. The bias is documented across model families.

**Control:** use a *different* model family for judging than for generation. If your caption chain runs on Sonnet, judge with GPT-4o. If you can't easily switch (loopd already has both providers; this is straightforward), acknowledge the bias in the eval report.

For loopd: the provider-abstracted chain layer makes cross-provider judging cheap to implement.

### Other biases worth knowing

- **Refusal bias** — judges sometimes refuse to score outputs that contain edge content (rare in journaling but worth knowing for product apps).
- **Anchoring bias** — judge's score on output N can be anchored by output N-1 in batched evals.
- **Sycophancy** — when the input prompt contains "this is a great output, please score it," the judge tends to agree. Don't write rubrics that lead the judge.

### Where these matter most

The biases multiply in pairwise comparison (position + verbosity + self-preference all act simultaneously). They matter less in rubric scoring of a single output (no position bias because there's nothing to compare to). They matter least in exact-match scoring of discrete labels (no judge at all).

### This is what people mean by "the eval is not the truth"

The eval is a measurement *of the truth* mediated by a judge that has its own preferences. Treating the eval score as ground truth without controls means shipping whatever the judge happens to like. Production eval discipline is fighting the bias, not pretending it doesn't exist. Here's the picture.

## Elaborate

### Where this pattern comes from
LLM-as-judge biases were systematically documented around 2023 in papers like Zheng et al. "Judging LLM-as-a-Judge" and follow-up work measuring position, verbosity, and self-preference effects. Before that, many evaluation papers using LLM judges didn't control for these biases — and their results were partially confounded.

### The deeper principle
A measurement instrument has its own biases. Sometimes you can control them out; sometimes you must acknowledge them in the report. The principle generalises beyond LLMs to any human-or-model judge (rubric raters in education, jury panels in product reviews, A/B test designers).

### Where this breaks down
Bias controls can become security theater if applied without measurement. Running pairwise twice doesn't help if you only run it once on the variants you care about. Including "concise" in the rubric doesn't help if you weight conciseness at 1% of the total score.

### What to explore next
- [36-eval-methods](./36-eval-methods.md) → the methods that use LLM-as-judge
- Position bias paper (Wang et al. 2023) → the systematic measurement
- Anthropic's "Constitutional AI" → an alternative framing where the judge is constrained explicitly

## Tradeoffs

### Comparison table — bias-controlled vs naive judge

```
┌────────────────────────┬───────────────────────┬─────────────────────────┐
│ Cost dimension         │ Bias-controlled       │ Naive LLM-judge         │
├────────────────────────┼───────────────────────┼─────────────────────────┤
│ Judge calls per eval   │ 2× (position swap)    │ 1×                      │
│ Cost per eval run      │ ~2× the naive cost    │ baseline                │
│ Reproducibility        │ Higher                │ Lower (random pos.)     │
│ False positives on     │ Few                   │ Many (driven by bias)   │
│   prompt changes       │                       │                         │
│ Cross-model setup      │ Two providers needed  │ One provider             │
│ Eval-vs-prod alignment │ Higher                │ Lower                    │
└────────────────────────┴───────────────────────┴─────────────────────────┘
```

### Sub-block 1 — what bias-controlled judging gives up

Per-eval cost doubles (position-swap means each pairwise comparison runs twice). For loopd's planned eval volume (~30 entries × 4 variants × pairwise = ~180 comparisons × 2 = 360 judge calls per eval run), that's a meaningful cost — ~$1-2 per full eval on Sonnet judge, vs ~$0.50-1 naive.

### Sub-block 2 — what naive judging would cost

False positives and false negatives in prompt-change decisions. A "12% quality lift" that's actually 6% bias would lead to shipping a verbose, sycophantic prompt because the eval said it was better. Users would notice; trust in the eval would erode.

### Sub-block 3 — the breakpoint
Bias controls are basically always worth the doubling cost. The breakpoint isn't whether to control biases; it's how thoroughly. Position swap is cheap and high-impact; running cross-model judging is more involved but valuable for high-stakes evals.

### What wasn't actually a tradeoff
Treating the judge as ground truth was never an option once these biases were named. The literature is clear.

## Tech reference (industry pairing)

### Cross-model judging (Anthropic + OpenAI)

- **Codebase uses:** loopd already has both providers via `getProvider()`, so cross-model judging is a config flip.
- **Why it's here:** the cheapest self-preference bias mitigation.
- **Leading today:** cross-model judging — `adoption-leading` for production eval, 2026.
- **Why it leads:** zero new infra; minimal code change in a provider-abstracted codebase.
- **Runner-up:** ensemble of judges (multiple models, averaged) — `innovation-leading` for highest fidelity; pays off for high-stakes evals.

### Position-swap protocol

- **Codebase uses:** target plan in `[B3.3]`, `[B3.7]`.
- **Why it's here:** the simplest, most-cited bias mitigation in the LLM-as-judge literature.
- **Leading today:** position-swap — `adoption-leading`, 2026.
- **Why it leads:** mathematically sound (averages out the bias); cheap to implement (one extra call per pair); supported by published research.

## Summary

LLM-as-judge has three documented biases — position, verbosity, self-preference — that systematically distort scores. In loopd this is not yet implemented; when caption and aipe eval suites ship, they include the three controls explicitly. The constraint that makes bias controls mandatory is that uncontrolled judges drive false-positive ship decisions (a "12% lift" that's actually 6% bias). The cost being paid is roughly doubled eval cost (position swap doubles judge calls).

Key points to remember:
- Three biases: position, verbosity, self-preference.
- Controls: swap positions, include "concise" in rubric, cross-model judging.
- Calibrate judge against human ratings before trusting it.
- Costs ~2× the eval call volume; worth it.
- Without controls, you ship the judge's preferences, not the user's.

## Interview defense

### What an interviewer is really asking
"How do you control for LLM-judge biases?" tests whether the candidate knows the biases exist and has implemented controls. "How do you know the judge is reliable?" tests whether they've calibrated.

### Likely questions

  [mid] Q: What's position bias and how do you control for it?
  A: Position bias is the tendency of judge LLMs to prefer whichever output is shown first in a pairwise comparison — measured at 5-15% across models. The control is to run every comparison twice with positions swapped, then average the scores. If A wins position-1 and loses position-2, treat it as a tie — the apparent preference was position bias. The cost is doubled judge calls per comparison; the benefit is removing a systematic confound.
  Diagram:
  ```
  judge(A, B) → "A wins"
  judge(B, A) → "B wins" or "A wins"
  
  If positions decide: tie.
  If A wins both:      real preference.
  ```

  [senior] Q: Why use a different judge model than the generator model?
  A: Self-preference bias — judges tend to prefer outputs from models in their own family by 5-10% on identical-quality outputs. For loopd, caption runs on Sonnet; using Sonnet as judge biases the eval toward Sonnet-flavoured outputs. The fix is using GPT-4o as judge, which loopd can do trivially because the provider-abstracted chain layer already supports both. The remaining bias (cross-model agreement noise) is smaller and easier to detect through judge-human calibration.
  Diagram:
  ```
  Picked: cross-model judge          Suggested: same-model judge
  ──────────────────────             ──────────────────────────
  Sonnet generates → GPT-4o judges    Sonnet both
  Self-preference: ~0                 Self-preference: 5-10% bias
  Two provider setup                  One provider
  ```

  [arch] Q: At scale, how does bias-controlled eval economics shift?
  A: The 2× cost of position-swap matters more at scale. At 1000 entries × 4 variants × pairwise + rubric, judge calls are in the tens of thousands. Mitigation: position-swap on a sample (every 5th comparison) rather than every one; aggregate the sample's swap-deltas as the bias estimate. Verbosity bias is fixed once in the rubric and pays no per-call cost.
  Diagram:
  ```
  Today (small):   Swap every comparison. 2× cost.
  10× (medium):    Swap every Nth. 1.2× cost; bias estimated.
  100× (large):    Swap on sampled batches. 1.05× cost.
  ```

### The question candidates always dodge
"How do you know your judge actually agrees with users?" The honest answer: judge-human calibration. You hand-label some outputs the same way the rubric would and check agreement. If agreement is below 80%, the judge is too noisy and the rubric/model needs work — using the judge anyway means shipping noise.

```
Picked: calibrate before trusting       Suggested: trust the judge blindly
─────────────────────────────             ─────────────────────────────
20 outputs hand-labelled                  No baseline
Agreement ≥ 80% required                  Eval ≠ user preference
Right at "we use this for ship"           Right at "we explore patterns"
```

### One-line anchors
- Three biases: position, verbosity, self-preference.
- Controls cost 2× judge calls; ship the controls.
- Different judge model from generator model.
- Include "concise" in rubric; don't reward verbosity.
- Calibrate against humans before trusting the judge.
